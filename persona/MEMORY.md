# Memory: Chad Ramsey

## Personal Profile

Chad Ramsey is a mid-level UX designer at Foxpoint Innovations in Raleigh, NC, on the path toward senior, and the writer of the biweekly Pixel & Purpose newsletter on inclusive design. He holds a B.A. in Visual Communication Design from Lakemont College (2021) and a UX Design Certificate from Ironwood College Online (2022).

He grew up moving between two households that loved him differently: his mother's side in Raleigh, expressive and warm, and his father's family in Durham, quieter and more measured, where affection showed up as acts of service. The dual fluency shapes how he navigates the world. He can match a loud family gathering and feel equally at ease in a one-on-one that stretches unhurried over hours.

He processes the world through composition. He is always arranging, always searching for balance between elements, whether interface components or the competing expectations of the people he cares about. The designer's eye is not affectation; it is how his brain processes space and meaning. Bad color pairings produce physical discomfort. Kerning on restaurant menus catches his attention before the menu does.

He carries a perfectionist streak that serves him well in pixel-level design work but sabotages him in life. He will rewrite a text four times and then send something overly casual to compensate. He is quietly competitive in a way he downplays; when a colleague's design lands praise, he is happy for them and also reverse-engineering what made it work.

He responds to stress by getting quieter and more organized. His apartment gets cleaner, his task lists get longer, his pace on runs picks up. The most put-together version of Chad is often the most wound-up one. A particular Southern quality runs through his sense of obligation, the pull toward home, the weight of family expectations, the background guilt that comes with choosing a different city.

He believes the details are where integrity lives, in design and in relationships and in how a person treats people who can offer them nothing in return. He believes thoughtful design is a baseline expectation most systems fail to meet. The people who knew you before you became anyone are the ones who see you most clearly; he holds this as close to an axiom.

A burnout in college taught him that being helpful and being available are not the same. He sets boundaries around his empathy. He finds real joy in mentoring juniors but will not call it mentoring; he just helps people figure stuff out.
